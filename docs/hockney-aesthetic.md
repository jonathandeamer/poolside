# Hockney Tribute Aesthetic

This document describes the visual and artistic direction for the rice. It is not a technical plan
for Hyprland, Waybar, colors, widgets, or scripts. Treat it as the source text for the feeling of
the desktop before translating anything into implementation.

David Hockney (1937-2026) died on 11 June 2026. This rice should be a tribute to the clarity,
curiosity, warmth, and visual pleasure of his work, especially the Southern California paintings of
the 1960s represented in `assets/hockney/`.

The aim is not to copy Hockney's paintings or turn the desktop into a gallery wall. The aim is to
absorb a way of seeing: bright light, still water, flat architecture, disciplined composition,
careful looking, and moments of motion suspended inside a calm world.

## Core Mood

The mood is sunlit, composed, and observant.

It should feel warm but not nostalgic, luxurious but not glossy, playful but not cute. Hockney's
California is not generic beach culture. It is a made world of modern houses, private pools, painted
lawns, social intimacy, glass panes, striped shutters, quiet furniture, and sudden water.

The desktop should eventually feel like sitting near a pool in sharp afternoon light: mostly still,
with one or two precise signs of life.

## Light

Light is the central material.

The reference paintings use broad fields of clear daylight rather than moody atmosphere. Shadows are
legible, directional, and structural. Reflections appear as marks on glass or water, not as soft
cinematic glow. The light is clean enough to flatten objects into shapes, but not so harsh that it
removes tenderness.

Important qualities:

- Sun-bleached clarity
- Hard-edged shadows
- White reflections on glass
- Pale walls and poolside concrete
- Cool blue air
- Bright color held inside stable geometry

Avoid a dark, neon, vaporwave, synthwave, or sunset interpretation. The emotional key is daylight.

## Color

The palette should be drawn from acrylic brightness and California air, not from digital gradients.

Recurring colors in the references:

- Pool cyan and turquoise
- Clear sky blue
- Pale cream and warm white
- Stucco pink and dusty rose
- Lawn green and palm green
- Charcoal glass and shaded interiors
- Soft gray concrete
- Lemon yellow
- Coral, rust, and narrow red-orange borders
- Occasional lavender, violet, and deep blue accents

The colors can be intense, but they should remain clean and separate. Hockney often lets one color
own a plane: blue as sky, blue as pool, pink as wall, green as lawn. The power comes from placement
and contrast, not from blending.

Avoid muddy palettes, heavy monochrome themes, overused beige minimalism, and purple-blue gradient
dominance.

## Composition

The strongest compositional idea is disciplined stillness interrupted by a sign of movement.

In `A Bigger Splash`, the building, pool, sky, and diving board are calm, simple planes. The splash
is the one turbulent event. In `A Lawn Being Sprinkled`, the lawn and fence form a strict field,
while the sprinkler cones make temporary water visible. In `Peter Getting Out of Nick's Pool`, the
architecture is a frame around a body and a pool surface full of looping lines.

Useful compositional principles:

- Large quiet fields
- Strong horizontal bands
- Rectangular modernist structure
- A shallow stage-like sense of space
- Thin framing borders
- Objects placed with deliberate spacing
- One expressive gesture inside an otherwise ordered scene
- Negative space that feels intentional

The rice should eventually inherit this restraint. The tribute should not become dense ornament.

## Surface And Line

Hockney's surfaces are clean but not sterile.

There are flat acrylic planes, but also hand-marked grass, brushed walls, wavering water lines,
striped shutters, patterned upholstery, and glass reflections. The paintings often hold geometry
and handwork in the same frame.

Important line languages:

- Straight architectural edges
- Thin verticals from posts, panes, trees, and furniture
- Horizontal bands from roofs, lawns, waterlines, and shutters
- White squiggles for water and reflected light
- Repeated short marks for grass and texture
- Diagonal glints across glass
- Narrow colored borders that acknowledge the picture as a picture

This should feel drawn, not rendered. Precision is welcome, but a little human unevenness is part of
the tribute.

## Space

The space is shallow and staged.

These images rarely create deep atmospheric perspective. They place figures, furniture, buildings,
pools, plants, and sculptures in clear layers. A house facade can feel like a backdrop. A room can
feel like a stage. A lawn can become a patterned field. The viewer is often held at a calm distance,
looking into a scene that has been arranged for attention.

That staged quality matters. It gives the work its coolness and its intimacy at the same time.

## People And Domesticity

The reference set is not just pools and architecture. It is also social space.

`Christopher Isherwood and Don Bachardy` and `American Collectors` show people arranged among books,
chairs, sculptures, shutters, plants, and light. The people are composed, but the paintings are not
cold. They treat domestic rooms and private patios as places where relationships, taste, sexuality,
friendship, and self-invention become visible.

The tribute should preserve that humane quality. It should not reduce Hockney to a pool icon or a
color palette. The aesthetic includes:

- Ease
- Queer openness
- Social intelligence
- Domestic confidence
- Attention to ordinary objects
- A belief that looking closely is worthwhile

## Water

Water is both subject and problem.

The pool paintings are not only about swimming pools as lifestyle objects. They are about how to
represent something unstable: reflection, ripple, transparency, eruption, spray. Hockney turns water
into signs: white loops, suspended splashes, pale cones, flickers, and broken surface patterns.

The best tribute to this is not literal pool decoration everywhere. It is the idea of visible motion
held inside structure.

## Artistic Position

The aesthetic sits between Pop, modernism, portraiture, stage design, and careful realism.

Hockney's 1960s California paintings borrow from modernist architecture, commercial image culture,
Renaissance staging, photographic composition, and the bright flatness of acrylic paint. They are
accessible without being simple. They look easy because they are so well organized.

This matters for the rice: the tribute should be generous and visually direct, not obscure. It
should reward looking, but it should not require explanation before it gives pleasure.

## What To Avoid

Avoid these false readings:

- Generic tropical wallpaper
- Cyberpunk pool party
- Vaporwave nostalgia
- Overly glossy luxury branding
- Dark glass minimalism
- Excessive gradients
- Random pastel decoration
- Literal copies of the paintings as UI assets
- Busy collage without compositional discipline
- Treating Hockney as only pools, palm trees, and blue

The tribute should be specific: California light, modernist order, domestic intimacy, painterly
surface, and one suspended event.

## Working Sentence

A calm, sunlit desktop world of pool blue, stucco pink, lawn green, pale concrete, glass shadows,
thin borders, and hand-drawn water marks: modern, warm, observant, and quietly alive.
